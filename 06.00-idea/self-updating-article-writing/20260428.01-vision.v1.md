## 🎯 The problem

> **Scope note.** This vision defines the **quality standards, governance rules, and design principles** for maintaining published articles and documentation. It does not prescribe specific implementations — it is the reference standard that article-writing PE artifacts (agents, prompts, skills, workflows) are evaluated against and improved toward. PE artifact maintenance itself is the PE self-update engine's responsibility.

### Two distinct maintenance concerns

The Learning Hub has two things that need maintaining, and they degrade through different forces:

**Concern 1: PE artifacts that govern writing** — instructions, context files, agents, prompts, skills, templates, and workflows. These degrade through platform drift (VS Code/Copilot changes), standards drift (MWSG/Diátaxis updates), and internal consistency drift (cross-artifact contradictions).

**Concern 2: Published articles produced by those PE artifacts** — the actual content readers consume. These degrade through factual staleness, link rot, series incoherence, coverage gaps, and standards non-compliance.

| Concern | Governed by | Maintained by | Purpose of governance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PE artifacts** (instructions, context files, agents, prompts, skills, templates) | [PE self-update vision](../self-updating-prompt-engineering/20260428.01-vision.v7.md) | PE self-update engine | Define standards PE artifacts must meet; engine evaluates and improves them |
| **Published articles** (the content readers see) | **This vision** | Article-writing PE artifacts (evaluated and improved by the PE engine) | Define quality standards articles must meet; PE artifacts implement the maintenance |

**This vision is the reference point.** Article-writing PE artifacts — research agents, validation agents, builder agents, review prompts, quality skills — exist to serve the standards defined here. The PE self-update engine evaluates and improves those artifacts against this vision. When an agent doesn't effectively detect factual staleness, or a skill doesn't efficiently check readability, the PE engine uses this vision's quality definitions to identify the gap and improve the artifact.

**This vision does NOT prescribe implementations.** It doesn't say "use agent X" or "run prompt Y." It defines what article quality means, what degradation looks like, how to classify change risk, and what governance constraints apply. Any implementation that achieves these standards — whether through current agents, future agents, MCP tools, or manual workflows — is valid.

### How articles degrade

Published articles degrade through five forces:

**1. Factual staleness.** Technology evolves. An article about "VS Code 1.100 features" becomes outdated when VS Code 1.115 ships. Code examples that worked with .NET 8 may need changes for .NET 10. API endpoints change, tools get deprecated, best practices shift.

**2. Link rot.** External URLs break, move, or get restructured. Internal cross-references break when articles are renamed or reorganized. A broken link in a reference section undermines the article's credibility and the reader's ability to verify claims.

**3. Series incoherence.** When one article in a series is updated independently, the series as a whole can lose consistency: terminology drifts between articles, progression assumptions break, structural echo violations appear, cross-references point to outdated section headings.

**4. Coverage gaps.** New topics emerge within an article's declared scope that the article doesn't cover. A prompt engineering article written before MCP existed has a coverage gap. An authentication article written before passkeys were mainstream has a coverage gap. The article's scope hasn't changed, but the territory it should cover has expanded.

**5. Standards non-compliance.** Writing standards evolve (MWSG updates, Diátaxis refinements, WCAG changes). When standards change, existing articles may no longer comply — not because the articles changed, but because the bar moved. An article using "click" instead of "select" was once fine; after the MWSG adopted input-neutral terminology, it became non-compliant.

These forces share a critical property: **articles don't fail loudly when they degrade**. A stale article still renders, still shows up in search, still gets read. The reader experiences degradation as confusion, frustration, or wasted time — not as a clear error signal. This silent degradation is the primary motivation for autonomous maintenance.

### The implementation gap

Article quality dimensions are well-defined (7 validation dimensions, 4 series-level dimensions, 5-signal freshness model). Article-writing PE artifacts exist to assess and repair articles. Review workflows specify when and how to review. SLA tiers define response targets.

What's missing is **autonomous orchestration governed by explicit quality standards**. Manual maintenance — humans triggering reviews when they remember to — doesn't scale beyond a handful of articles and relies on human memory to track degradation. The quality standards live implicitly in scattered artifacts — validation criteria, style guides, instruction files, workflows. This vision makes them explicit, gives them governance rules, and provides the reference standard that the PE engine uses to evaluate and improve article-writing artifacts toward peak robustness, quality, and efficiency.

## 💡 The goal

Build a system that continuously maintains published articles, article series, and documentation at peak quality across five dimensions:

| Quality dimension | Definition | Key indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Verified** | Every factual claim is backed by current, accessible sources | Sources cited, claims checked against current state, AI provenance tagged |
| **Reliable** | Content produces consistent, trustworthy outcomes for readers | Code examples work, procedures complete successfully, links resolve |
| **Readable** | Content meets quantitative readability targets | Flesch 50–70, FK 8–10, sentence length 15–25, active voice 75–85% |
| **Understandable** | Content is appropriate for its declared audience | Jargon explained, prerequisites stated, progressive complexity, tables introduced |
| **Up to date** | Content reflects the current state of its subject | Freshness score ≥50, versions current, no deprecated references |

### The article maintenance cycle

The system follows a four-phase cycle. Each phase defines WHAT must happen, not which tool does it:

```
    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                      │
    ▼                                                      │
┌───────────┐    ┌───────────┐    ┌───────────┐    ┌───────┴───┐
│  DETECT   │───▶│  ASSESS   │───▶│  PROPOSE  │───▶│  EXECUTE  │
│           │    │           │    │           │    │           │
│ Freshness │    │ Quality   │    │ Scoped    │    │ Verified  │
│ scoring   │    │ dimension │    │ change    │    │ changes   │
│ Link      │    │ assessment│    │ plan per  │    │ applied   │
│ checks    │    │ by cost   │    │ article   │    │ Post-     │
│ Source    │    │ tier      │    │ Human or  │    │ change    │
│ changes   │    │ Series    │    │ auto per  │    │ validation│
│ Triggers  │    │ integrity │    │ gradient  │    │ confirmed │
└───────────┘    └───────────┘    └───────────┘    └───────────┘
```

### The autonomy gradient for article changes

Autonomy is calibrated to **change impact on readers**:

| Autonomy level | Scope | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Autonomous** | Non-content, low risk | Fix broken link with valid replacement, update bottom validation metadata, correct obvious typo |
| **Autonomous with notification** | Content-preserving, verified | Update version number with confirmed new version, fix code example to match current API (verified), add missing alt text to image |
| **Human review required** | Content-altering or medium confidence | Rewrite section for updated best practice, add new coverage within scope, modify factual claims based on source changes |
| **Human-only** | Structural or strategic | Deprecate an article, split/merge articles, change article scope, alter series architecture |

### Key definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Article** | A published markdown document in a content area (01.00-news through 99.00-temp) — the primary output readers consume |
| **Article series** | A group of related articles with shared scope, cross-references, and progressive structure |
| **Freshness score** | Composite metric (0–100) from 5 signals: days since validation, version currency, link health, open issues, code validity (defined in `02-validation-criteria.md`) |
| **Quality dimension** | One of the five article quality targets: verified, reliable, readable, understandable, up to date |
| **Content change** | A modification to what readers see — factual claims, code examples, procedures, explanations |
| **Metadata change** | A modification to validation timestamps, freshness scores, or tracking fields — invisible to readers |

## 🔄 Options analysis

Three architectural approaches for maintaining published articles:

### Option A: Scheduled batch reviews

A periodic (weekly/monthly) process runs validation across all articles, produces a report, and queues fixes.

**Strengths:**
- Simple implementation — a scheduled prompt that runs validation
- Catches degradation before it becomes critical
- Report-based — human reviews findings before fixes are applied

**Weaknesses:**
- Batch processing is expensive — validates everything, including articles that haven't changed
- No event-driven response — a broken link waits until the next batch run
- All-or-nothing — can't prioritize P0 issues over P4 enhancements
- Batch reports can be overwhelming — 50 findings across 30 articles is hard to triage

**Verdict:** Better than manual, but inefficient. Wastes tokens on healthy articles and misses urgent issues between batch runs. A useful fallback for scheduled reviews but not the primary strategy.

### Option B: Event-driven maintenance with autonomous execution (recommended)

The system continuously monitors articles through freshness scoring and external signals. When degradation is detected, it triggers scoped assessment using the existing agents, proposes targeted fixes, and executes low-risk fixes autonomously while escalating high-risk changes.

**Strengths:**
- Responds to degradation when it happens, not when the next batch runs
- Efficient — only assesses articles with detected signals, not the entire inventory
- SLA-aware — P0 issues (security, accuracy) trigger faster response than P4 (style)
- Uses existing agents — no new tools to build, just orchestration
- Autonomy gradient — broken link fixes are autonomous; factual rewrites escalate
- Quality feedback loop — article outcomes signal PE artifact issues to the PE engine

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires orchestration infrastructure (trigger wiring, signal processing)
- Autonomous execution carries risk — incorrect fixes degrade articles
- Depends on reliable freshness scoring and signal detection

**Verdict:** Recommended. This approach combines the efficiency of targeted responses with the safety of the autonomy gradient, using tools that already exist.

### Option C: Full article rewrite pipeline

When an article's freshness drops below a threshold, regenerate it from scratch using builder capabilities with current sources.

**Strengths:**
- Guarantees current content
- Avoids incremental drift — each version is generated fresh

**Weaknesses:**
- Extremely expensive — full article generation costs far more than targeted fixes
- Loses editorial voice and human-contributed nuance
- Author attribution becomes meaningless
- Doesn't preserve deliberate editorial choices
- Overkill for minor degradation (broken link → full rewrite?)

**Verdict:** Rejected. Regeneration should be a last resort for articles with freshness score <25, not the default maintenance strategy.

## ⚙️ The rationale

### Inherited governance patterns

The article maintenance system inherits governance infrastructure from the PE v7 engine. These patterns apply to article changes the same way they apply to PE artifact changes (not repeated in full — see [PE vision](../self-updating-prompt-engineering/20260428.01-vision.v7.md)):

| Pattern | Article-writing application |
|---|---|
| **Autonomy gradient** (R-G1) | Calibrated to reader impact — broken link fix is autonomous; factual rewrite requires human review |
| **Multi-pass validation** (R-L2) | No article change is committed without validation capability confirming the change doesn't introduce regressions |
| **Decomposition** (R-P2) | Large updates (series-wide terminology fix) are decomposed into per-article changes |
| **Metadata contracts** (R-S1) | Article validation metadata (bottom YAML) tracks freshness, last validated, validation outcomes |
| **Progressive learning** (R-G3) | Track which maintenance actions improved article quality and which introduced regressions |
| **Loop stability** | Article maintenance converges — successive cycles produce fewer findings, not more |
| **Deterministic where possible** (R-P1) | Link checking, freshness scoring, reference resolution are deterministic |
| **Iteration budget** | Bounded autonomous article changes per cycle (default: 10) |

### Article-writing-specific rationales

| ID | Rationale | Design implication |
|---|---|---|
| **R-A1-reader-impact-first** | **Article changes are assessed by reader impact, not structural risk.** PE artifact changes are assessed by blast radius through the artifact graph. Article changes are assessed by how they affect the reader's experience — a factual error in a widely-read article is higher impact than a structural issue in a rarely-visited one. | Autonomy routing considers article traffic/importance alongside change type. High-visibility articles get more conservative thresholds. |
| **R-A2-verify-before-change** | **Every factual change must be verified against current sources before application.** LLMs can confidently "fix" an article by introducing hallucinated "corrections." No factual change without source verification — the hallucination resistance principle from the research vision applies. | Research capabilities verify facts before builder capabilities apply changes. Source verification is not optional, regardless of confidence. |
| **R-A3-preserve-editorial-voice** | **Maintenance preserves the author's editorial choices.** Articles have voice, style, and structure decisions made by their author. Maintenance fixes what's wrong (stale facts, broken links, non-compliant formatting) without rewriting what's deliberately chosen (explanatory approach, example selection, narrative structure). | Changes target specific degradation signals. "Rewrite for clarity" is not a maintenance action — it's a human editorial decision. Builder capabilities receive change-scoped instructions, not "improve this article." |
| **R-A4-series-coherence** | **Series integrity is maintained as a unit, not per-article.** Updating one article in a series can break the series as a whole — terminology drifts, cross-references become inconsistent, progression assumptions break. When maintenance touches a series article, it must assess the series-level impact. | Series-level validation (4 additional dimensions from `02-validation-criteria.md`) runs after any article in a series is modified. Changes that improve one article but break series coherence are blocked. |
| **R-A5-freshness-driven** | **Freshness scoring is the primary detection signal.** The 5-signal freshness model (days since validation, version currency, link health, open issues, code validity) already exists and provides a deterministic, quantitative trigger for maintenance. Articles dropping below threshold trigger scoped assessment. | Freshness scoring runs continuously (or on schedule). Threshold crossings trigger assessment. The threshold is configurable per content type (technical docs need fresher content than concept articles). |
| **R-A6-quality-feedback-to-pe** | **Article outcomes signal PE artifact effectiveness.** When articles consistently fail on a specific validation dimension, the problem may be in the articles OR in the writing rules that produced them. Article maintenance doesn't fix PE artifacts — it signals the PE engine to investigate. | Validation outcome patterns are logged. When a pattern suggests a PE artifact issue (e.g., >50% of articles fail readability on introductions), the signal is sent to the PE engine as a detection trigger. |
| **R-A7-capability-separation** | **The system requires three distinct capabilities: research (analysis), validation (assessment), and building (execution).** These capabilities may be implemented as agents, skills, MCP tools, or manual workflows — the vision defines the capability contract, not the implementation. Each capability has a defined role: research analyzes quality gaps and verifies facts; validation scores articles against quality dimensions; building applies verified corrections. | Implementations must provide all three capability types. The PE engine evaluates whether each capability effectively serves the standards defined in this vision. |
| **R-A8-cost-stratified-processing** | **Quality dimensions have vastly different processing costs — run cheap checks first, expensive checks only when needed.** Link checking is an HTTP request (near-zero cost). Readability scoring is arithmetic on word counts (near-zero). Factual accuracy verification requires fetching external sources and LLM reasoning (high cost). Understandability assessment requires deep LLM judgment about audience calibration (high cost). Running all dimensions at the same depth for every article wastes tokens on healthy content. | Validation is organized into cost tiers. Tier 0 (deterministic) runs first and filters out articles that don't need LLM assessment. Tier 1 (LLM-lightweight) runs on articles with deterministic findings. Tier 2 (LLM-deep) runs only when the trigger signal requires reasoning-dependent assessment. Each quality dimension declares its cost tier. |
| **R-A9-dimension-isolation** | **Each quality dimension is an independently-assessable capability.** Readability, grammar, structure, factual accuracy, logical flow, completeness, understandability — each has its own processing type, cost profile, cadence, and trigger conditions. Implementations should encapsulate each dimension as a separately-invocable unit (via skills, prompts, tools, or any mechanism) so the orchestrator can compose exactly the checks needed at minimum cost. | Quality dimensions are not assessed monolithically. Each dimension has a declared cost tier, a defined trigger condition, and an independent pass/fail result. The orchestrator composes dimensions by trigger type — a broken-link trigger doesn't invoke readability assessment. |

### Conflict resolution

| Conflict | Resolution |
|---|---|
| R-A1-reader-impact-first vs R-G1-autonomy-gradient (inherited) | R-A1 constrains R-G1 — reader-facing articles get more conservative autonomy thresholds than PE artifacts. A change that would be autonomous for a PE artifact may require notification for a high-visibility article. |
| R-A2-verify-before-change vs efficiency | R-A2 wins unconditionally — source verification is never skipped to save tokens. An unverified "correction" that introduces a factual error causes more harm than the stale content it replaced. |
| R-A3-preserve-editorial-voice vs R-A5-freshness-driven | R-A3 constrains R-A5 — freshness-driven maintenance fixes degradation without rewriting deliberate editorial choices. A low freshness score triggers scoped fixes, not wholesale rewrites. |
| R-A4-series-coherence vs R-A1-reader-impact | R-A4 is a guard on R-A1 — a change with positive reader impact on one article is blocked if it degrades series coherence. Series integrity takes precedence over per-article improvement. |
| R-A8-cost-stratified vs R-A2-verify-before-change | R-A2 wins unconditionally — cost stratification determines WHEN to verify, not WHETHER to verify. Tier 0 checks may determine that verification is needed; verification itself is never skipped to save cost. |

## 🏗️ The vision

A self-updating article-writing system **continuously monitors published articles for quality degradation**, **uses existing article-writing agents to assess and repair degradation**, **executes verified corrections autonomously when reader risk is low**, and **feeds quality patterns back to the PE engine to improve the rules that govern writing**.

### Detect

*Uses: freshness scoring (deterministic), link checking (deterministic), source monitoring (LLM-assisted), series consistency checks (mixed)*

Six detection signals trigger scoped assessment:

| Signal | Detection method | Trigger condition |
|---|---|---|
| **Freshness score drop** | 5-signal composite scoring from `02-validation-criteria.md` | Article drops below configured threshold (default: 50) |
| **Broken link detected** | Deterministic URL checking | Any broken external or internal link |
| **Source content changed** | Content hash comparison on cited sources | A cited source's content has changed since last validation |
| **Technology version update** | Release note monitoring (shared with PE engine) | A technology referenced in articles has a new version |
| **Series article modified** | File watcher / git hook | An article in a series is modified — trigger series-level check |
| **Scheduled review due** | Time-based per content type | Review cycle expired (90 days for technical, annual for concepts) |

**Freshness scoring.** The existing 5-signal model from `02-validation-criteria.md` is the primary detection mechanism:

| Signal | Weight | Fresh (80–100) | Aging (50–79) | Stale (25–49) | Critical (0–24) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Days since validation | 30% | <30 days | 30–90 days | 91–180 days | >180 days |
| Version currency | 25% | Current | One behind | Two behind | Deprecated |
| Link health | 15% | 100% valid | >90% valid | >75% valid | <75% valid |
| Open issues | 15% | 0 | 1–2 | 3–5 | >5 |
| Code validity | 15% | All pass | Minor issues | Some broken | Major failures |

**Priority routing.** Detection signals map to SLA tiers for response urgency:

| SLA tier | Category | Response target | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **P0** | Security or accuracy error | 4 hours | Incorrect security guidance, wrong API credentials example |
| **P1** | Broken code or links | 24 hours | Code example throws error, reference link 404 |
| **P2** | Outdated versions | 1 week | Article references .NET 8 when .NET 10 is current |
| **P3** | Style or readability | 30 days | Non-compliant with updated MWSG guidance |
| **P4** | Enhancement | Next scheduled review | Better example available, additional explanation would help |

### Assess

*Relies on: R-A8-cost-stratified-processing, R-A9-dimension-isolation, R-A4-series-coherence*

When detection triggers assessment, quality checks run in **cost order** — cheapest first, most expensive only when needed. Series checks are **interleaved with per-article checks by cost tier**, not deferred to the end — because a cheap series-level finding (orphan article, broken series chain, scope overlap) can invalidate expensive per-article work.

**Step 1: Scope the assessment.** What triggered it? Which articles are affected? If it's a series article, which other articles in the series need checking?

**Step 2: Run Tier 0 (deterministic) checks — per-article AND series-structural.** These are near-zero cost and produce definitive pass/fail results:

*Per-article Tier 0:*

| Check | Processing | Cost | What it catches |
|---|---|---|---|
| Link health | HTTP status codes | ~0 | Broken links (404, 5xx, redirects) |
| Freshness scoring | Date arithmetic, version comparison | ~0 | Stale content (days since validation, version currency) |
| Structure validation | Markdown parsing, pattern matching | ~0 | Missing required sections, heading hierarchy violations, emoji H2 absence |
| Metadata completeness | YAML parsing | ~0 | Missing top/bottom YAML fields, invalid formats |
| Reference classification | Pattern matching | ~0 | Unclassified references, 📕 markers in published content |
| Image alt text | Regex | ~0 | Images without alt attributes |
| Code block language | Regex | ~0 | Code blocks without language identifiers |

*Series Tier 0 (if article belongs to a series):*

| Check | Processing | Cost | What it catches |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cross-reference integrity | Deterministic path check | ~0 | Broken prev/next links, orphan articles, dead-end articles |
| Series navigation chain | Path + sequence check | ~0 | Gaps in prev/next chain, circular links, unreachable articles |
| Scope overlap detection | YAML `scope.covers` comparison | ~0 | Two articles covering the same topics (duplication signal) |
| Diátaxis type distribution | YAML field comparison | ~0 | All articles same type, missing fundamental types for series size |

**Series-structural gate.** Series Tier 0 findings can **abort or redirect** expensive per-article work:

| Series Tier 0 finding | Impact on per-article assessment | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Article is an orphan (unreachable from siblings) | Skip Tier 1–2 per-article; escalate series restructuring to human | Per-article quality work is wasted if the article's role in the series is undefined |
| Scope overlap with sibling (>60% topic overlap) | Skip Tier 2 completeness/gap analysis; escalate merge decision to human | Gap analysis on overlapping articles produces contradictory recommendations |
| Missing Diátaxis type (series needs a type it doesn't have) | Flag as series-level coverage gap — don't burden existing articles | The gap is a missing article, not a problem with existing articles |
| Broken navigation chain | Autonomous fix (deterministic) — then continue per-article | Navigation is fixable independently; doesn't affect per-article content quality |

If no series-structural findings exist, per-article assessment continues normally. If gating findings exist, the assessment redirects — saving expensive per-article checks on articles whose series role is uncertain.

Tier 0 findings (both per-article and series) are immediately actionable. If Tier 0 finds nothing and no trigger signal requires deeper assessment, the article passes without consuming LLM tokens.

**Step 3: Run Tier 1 (LLM-lightweight) checks.** These require LLM processing but are single-pass, focused, and relatively cheap:

| Check | Processing | Cost | What it catches |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grammar and mechanics | LLM single-pass scan | Low | Spelling errors, punctuation issues, inconsistent capitalization, contraction usage |
| Readability scoring | LLM-assisted sentence analysis + formula | Low | Flesch/FK scores, sentence length distribution, active voice percentage |
| Standards compliance | LLM comparison against rules | Low-medium | UI terminology ("click" vs "select"), formatting violations, missing contractions |

Tier 1 checks run when: (a) Tier 0 found structural issues that suggest broader problems, (b) the trigger signal is a standards update, or (c) scheduled review requires full validation.

**Step 4: Run Tier 2 (LLM-deep) checks — per-article AND series-semantic.** These require multi-source reasoning, external fetches, or deep judgment:

*Per-article Tier 2:*

| Check | Processing | Cost | What it catches |
|---|---|---|---|
| Factual accuracy | Source fetching + LLM comparison | High | Outdated claims, incorrect technical details, deprecated guidance |
| Logical flow | LLM deep reading + structural analysis | Medium-high | Concepts used before introduction, prerequisite gaps, contradictions |
| Understandability | LLM audience-calibration judgment | Medium-high | Audience mismatch, unexplained jargon, sudden complexity jumps, missing table introductions |
| Completeness / gap analysis | LLM scope comparison + source research | High | Missing topics within declared scope, outdated coverage |
| Progressive disclosure | LLM structural reasoning | Medium | Information ordering, complexity gradient, cognitive load spikes |
| Visual/diagram adequacy | LLM content-to-visual assessment | Medium | Missing diagrams for complex concepts, outdated screenshots |

*Series Tier 2 (if article belongs to a series and no series-structural gate blocked it):*

| Check | Processing | Cost | What it catches |
|---|---|---|---|
| Terminology consistency | LLM comparison across articles | Medium-high | Terminology drift between sibling articles |
| Progression coherence | LLM structural analysis | Medium-high | Broken prerequisite chains, duplicated content, complexity regression |
| Structural echo | LLM pattern comparison | Medium | Same-type articles with different section patterns |

Series Tier 2 checks run alongside per-article Tier 2 — both require LLM reasoning and benefit from per-article Tier 0/1 findings as input. Series-semantic checks use per-article assessment results (readability scores, structural findings) to inform cross-article comparison.

**Step 5: Classify findings.** Each finding gets a severity and impact assessment:

| Severity | Criteria | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **CRITICAL** | Reader harm or fundamentally incorrect information | Security vulnerability in code example, factually wrong claim, completely broken procedure |
| **HIGH** | Significant quality degradation | Multiple broken links, outdated code that won't compile, major coverage gap |
| **MEDIUM** | Noticeable quality issue | Readability below threshold, minor factual inaccuracy, missing prerequisite |
| **LOW** | Cosmetic or marginal improvement | Style non-compliance, slightly outdated example, wording improvement |

### Propose

*Relies on: R-G1-autonomy-gradient, R-A1-reader-impact-first, R-A4-series-coherence*

For each finding, the system generates a scoped change proposal:

**Evidence-based.** Every proposal cites:
1. What detection signal triggered it
2. What research capability found
3. What validation capability scored
4. What specific change is proposed
5. What the reader impact is (positive: what improves; negative: what might regress)

**Per-change autonomy routing:**

| Change type | Autonomy level | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Fix broken link (replacement found and verified) | Autonomous | Deterministic, reader-positive, no content risk |
| Update validation metadata timestamps | Autonomous | Non-content, invisible to readers |
| Fix obvious typo | Autonomous | Content-preserving, deterministic |
| Update version number (source-verified) | Autonomous with notification | Factual, verifiable, reader-visible |
| Fix code example (tested against current API) | Autonomous with notification | Reader-visible but verifiable |
| Add missing alt text | Autonomous with notification | Accessibility improvement, content-preserving |
| Rewrite section for factual update | Human review | Content-altering, risk of voice/nuance loss |
| Add new coverage within scope | Human review | Content-expanding, requires editorial judgment |
| Restructure article | Human-only | Structural change, affects series coherence |
| Deprecate article | Human-only | Strategic decision |

**Series-aware proposals.** When a proposed change affects a series article, the proposal includes the series-level impact assessment. Changes that improve one article but degrade series coherence are flagged for human review, regardless of the per-article autonomy level.

### Execute

*Relies on: R-A2-verify-before-change, R-A3-preserve-editorial-voice, R-L2-self-correction*

**Pre-change:** Record article state (content hash, validation scores, freshness score).

**Apply:** The builder capability receives a **change-scoped instruction** — not "improve this article" but "fix the broken link in the References section: replace `old-url` with `new-url`" or "update the version number from 8.0 to 10.0 in the Prerequisites section, verified against the release notes."

**Post-change validation:** Validation capability runs on the modified article. The change is accepted only if:
- The target finding is resolved
- No new CRITICAL or HIGH findings are introduced
- Readability scores don't regress below thresholds
- Series-level dimensions (if applicable) don't regress

**Post-change metadata update:** Bottom validation YAML is updated with new timestamps, freshness scores, and validation outcomes.

**Rollback:** If post-change validation fails, the change is reverted to the pre-change state. The finding is re-classified with higher severity and escalated.

### Chain alignment for article cross-references

*Adapts: PE v7 R-S5-chain-alignment for article-level references*

Articles reference each other and external sources. These references degrade differently from PE artifact references:

| Reference type | Degradation mode | Detection | Maintenance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Series prev/next links** | Article renamed or moved | Deterministic path check | Autonomous fix |
| **Related article links** | Target article removed or restructured | Deterministic path check | Autonomous fix (if replacement found) or escalate |
| **External source citations** | URL breaks, content changes | HTTP check + content hash | Autonomous link fix; content change triggers re-assessment |
| **Code repository links** | Repo restructured, branch renamed | HTTP check | Autonomous fix (if redirect detected) or escalate |
| **Image references** | Image moved or deleted | File existence check | Escalate (images require editorial judgment) |

**Series cross-reference integrity.** Within a series, cross-references must form a consistent navigation structure. The maintenance system checks:
- Prev/next links form a connected chain
- No orphan articles (reachable but not linked from any sibling)
- No dead-end articles (linked to but not linking to siblings)
- Terminology used in cross-references matches the target article's current title/headings

### Quality dimension architecture

*Relies on: R-A8-cost-stratified-processing, R-A9-dimension-isolation, R-A7-capability-separation*

Article quality is not a single monolithic assessment. It decomposes into independently-assessable dimensions, each with its own processing type, cost profile, and trigger conditions. Implementations should encapsulate each dimension as a separately-invocable capability — via skills, prompts, agents, MCP tools, or any mechanism — so the orchestrator can compose exactly the checks needed.

#### Why per-dimension isolation

A monolithic "validate this article" pass is simple but wasteful: it runs grammar checks when the trigger was a broken link, and readability checks when the trigger was a version update. Per-dimension isolation enables:

- **Selective invocation** — only the dimensions relevant to the trigger are checked
- **Cost-appropriate processing** — deterministic dimensions don't consume LLM tokens
- **Independent caching** — a dimension validated within its cadence is skipped even if other dimensions need checking
- **Composable orchestration** — the orchestrator selects dimensions by trigger type, not by a fixed sequence

#### Quality dimension classification

Each quality dimension declares its cost tier, processing type, and trigger conditions:

| Quality dimension | Tier | Processing type | Invoked when |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Link health** | 0 | Deterministic (HTTP) | Every cycle — cheapest signal |
| **Freshness scoring** | 0 | Deterministic (arithmetic) | Every cycle — primary trigger |
| **Structure** | 0 | Deterministic (parsing) | Every cycle — catches structural drift |
| **Metadata completeness** | 0 | Deterministic (YAML parse) | Every cycle |
| **Reference classification** | 0 | Deterministic (pattern) | Every cycle |
| **Series: cross-reference integrity** | 0 | Deterministic (path check) | Every cycle for series articles — cheapest series signal |
| **Series: navigation chain** | 0 | Deterministic (sequence check) | Every cycle for series articles |
| **Series: scope overlap** | 0 | Deterministic (YAML comparison) | Every cycle for series articles |
| **Series: Diátaxis type distribution** | 0 | Deterministic (field comparison) | Every cycle for series articles |
| **Grammar and mechanics** | 1 | LLM single-pass | Tier 0 findings, scheduled review, or standards update |
| **Readability** | 1 | LLM-assisted + formula | Tier 0 findings, scheduled review |
| **Standards compliance** | 1 | LLM comparison | Standards update trigger |
| **Factual accuracy** | 2 | LLM + source fetching | Technology version update, source content change, scheduled review |
| **Logical flow** | 2 | LLM deep reading | Structural findings in Tier 0/1, scheduled review |
| **Understandability** | 2 | LLM judgment | Readability findings in Tier 1, scheduled review |
| **Completeness** | 2 | LLM + research | Coverage gap signal, scheduled review |
| **Progressive disclosure** | 2 | LLM structural reasoning | Readability or understandability findings |
| **Visual/diagram adequacy** | 2 | LLM content-to-visual assessment | Scheduled review, completeness findings |
| **Series: terminology consistency** | 2 | LLM cross-article | Series article modified, scheduled review |
| **Series: progression coherence** | 2 | LLM structural analysis | Series article modified, scheduled review |
| **Series: structural echo** | 2 | LLM pattern comparison | Series article modified, scheduled review |
| **Correlated topics** | 2 | LLM cross-domain | Coverage gap, scheduled review |
| **Publication readiness** | All | Composite gate | Before any autonomous content change is committed |

#### Dimension composition by trigger type

The orchestrator selects dimensions based on what triggered the assessment:

| Trigger | Dimensions invoked | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Broken link detected** | Link health (T0) + Series cross-refs (T0) → if cited source changed: Factual accuracy (T2) | Check series navigation alongside article links; source changes may affect facts |
| **Technology version update** | Freshness (T0) + Series scope overlap (T0) → Factual accuracy (T2) → Completeness (T2) | Scope overlap check prevents redundant updates across overlapping articles |
| **Scheduled review (technical)** | All T0 + Series T0 → if series-structural gate passes: Grammar (T1) + Readability (T1) → Factual accuracy (T2) + Completeness (T2) + Series terminology/echo (T2) | Full progressive; series-structural gate prevents wasted per-article work |
| **Scheduled review (concept)** | All T0 + Series T0 → Grammar (T1) + Readability (T1) → Understandability (T2) | Concept articles rarely have factual drift but may have clarity issues |
| **Standards update** | Structure (T0) → Standards compliance (T1) → Grammar (T1) | Standards changes affect formatting and terminology, rarely facts |
| **Series article modified** | Series T0 (cross-refs, chain, scope overlap, type distribution) → if no gate: Series T2 (terminology, progression, echo) | Cheap series checks first; expensive only if structure is sound |
| **Post-change verification** | Publication readiness (all tiers) | Full check after any modification |

#### Cost efficiency mechanisms

This architecture achieves cost efficiency through four mechanisms:

1. **Early termination.** If Tier 0 finds the article is healthy and no trigger signal requires deeper assessment, no LLM tokens are consumed.
2. **Series-structural gate.** Cheap series Tier 0 checks (cross-references, scope overlap, type distribution) can abort expensive per-article Tier 1–2 work when the article's series role is uncertain — preventing wasted effort on articles that may be restructured, merged, or repositioned.
3. **Selective depth.** A broken-link trigger invokes link health + possibly factual accuracy — not grammar, readability, or understandability. Only relevant dimensions are checked.
4. **Cached validation.** Per-dimension validation results are cached with content hashes. A dimension validated within its cadence and with unchanged content is skipped.

The net effect: a maintenance cycle across 100 articles may consume LLM tokens for only the 5–10 articles with detected degradation, and within those, only the dimensions with actual findings.

#### Three capability types

Implementations must provide three capability types, each serving a distinct role in the maintenance cycle:

| Capability | Cycle phase | Contract |
|---|---|---|
| **Research** (analysis) | Assess — deep analysis | Analyzes quality gaps, verifies facts against sources, assesses series integrity. Read-only — never modifies articles. |
| **Validation** (assessment) | Assess — scoring, Execute — confirmation | Scores articles against quality dimensions. Produces per-dimension pass/fail with severity. Read-only — never modifies articles. |
| **Building** (execution) | Execute — applying changes | Applies verified, change-scoped corrections. Receives specific instructions, not open-ended "improve" directives. |

How these capabilities are implemented — as agents, skills, MCP tools, prompt files, or manual workflows — is an implementation decision. The vision defines the capability contract; the PE engine evaluates whether implementations fulfill it effectively.

### Quality feedback loop to PE engine

*Relies on: R-A6-quality-feedback-to-pe, R-G3-progressive-learning*

Article validation outcomes contain signal about PE artifact effectiveness. The feedback loop connects article maintenance to PE artifact maintenance:

```
┌──────────────┐      ┌──────────────┐      ┌──────────────┐
│ PE artifacts │─────▶│ Articles     │─────▶│ Validation   │
│ (rules,      │      │ (content)    │      │ (outcomes)   │
│  standards)  │      │              │      │              │
└──────┬───────┘      └──────────────┘      └──────┬───────┘
       │                                           │
       │      ┌─────────────────────────┐          │
       │◀─────│ PE engine investigates  │◀─────────┘
       │      │ rule effectiveness      │  Signal: pattern
       │      └─────────────────────────┘  detected
       │
  PE engine
  updates rules
```

**Signal types from article outcomes → PE engine:**

| Pattern detected | Signal to PE engine | PE engine action |
|---|---|---|
| >50% of articles fail readability on introductions | "Investigate readability rules for introduction sections" | PE engine assesses whether the rule is too strict, unclear, or conflicting |
| Articles consistently use "click" despite "select" rule | "Investigate UI terminology rule effectiveness" | PE engine assesses whether the rule is impractical or insufficiently enforced |
| New articles consistently miss emoji H2 headings | "Investigate heading formatting rule propagation" | PE engine checks whether the rule is in the right instruction tier |
| Validation false positives on a specific dimension | "Investigate validation threshold calibration" | PE engine assesses whether the threshold needs adjustment |

**What article maintenance does NOT do:** It doesn't modify PE artifacts (instructions, context files, agents, prompts). It signals the PE engine, which assesses and acts through its own Detect → Assess → Propose → Execute cycle.

## 📋 Design principles

### Accuracy over everything

*Relies on: R-A2-verify-before-change*

A stale-but-accurate article is better than a current-but-wrong article. Every factual change is verified against authoritative sources before application. No exceptions. When verification is ambiguous, the change escalates to human review. When verification is impossible (source unavailable), the change is deferred, not guessed.

### Fix what's broken, preserve what's chosen

*Relies on: R-A3-preserve-editorial-voice*

Maintenance targets specific degradation — broken links, stale facts, non-compliant formatting. It doesn't rewrite articles for "improvement" or impose a different explanatory style. The author's editorial choices (structure, examples, narrative) are preserved unless they directly cause a quality failure. "This could be written better" is not a maintenance finding; "this code example throws an error" is.

### Series before articles

*Relies on: R-A4-series-coherence*

A change that improves one article but breaks the series is a net negative. Series-level consistency (terminology, progression, structural echo, cross-references) is checked after every change to a series article. When per-article improvement conflicts with series integrity, series integrity wins.

### Freshness as the heartbeat

*Relies on: R-A5-freshness-driven*

Freshness scoring runs continuously and provides the primary maintenance trigger. It's deterministic, quantitative, and already defined. Articles below the threshold get assessed; articles above it are left alone (unless another signal triggers assessment). This prevents both over-maintenance (constantly checking healthy articles) and under-maintenance (ignoring degrading articles).

### Three capabilities, not one monolith

*Relies on: R-A7-capability-separation*

Article maintenance requires three distinct capabilities: research (analysis), validation (assessment), and building (execution). These capabilities have different tool access requirements, different risk profiles, and different invocation patterns. Implementations that combine them into a single monolithic pass lose the ability to invoke each at the right time with the right scope. Implementations that separate them gain composability — the orchestrator runs research when facts need checking, validation when scores are needed, and building when corrections are verified.

### Cheap checks first, expensive checks only when needed

*Relies on: R-A8-cost-stratified-processing, R-A9-dimension-isolation*

Not all quality dimensions cost the same to assess. Link checking is an HTTP request. Readability is arithmetic. Understandability assessment requires deep LLM judgment. Running all dimensions at full depth for every article in every cycle is wasteful. Tier 0 (deterministic) runs first and cheaply; Tier 1 (LLM-lightweight) runs when Tier 0 finds issues or the trigger requires it; Tier 2 (LLM-deep) runs only for the specific dimensions the trigger demands. Per-dimension isolation lets the orchestrator compose exactly the checks needed — no more, no less.

### Signal, don't fix (for PE artifacts)

*Relies on: R-A6-quality-feedback-to-pe*

When article outcomes suggest a PE artifact problem, the maintenance system signals the PE engine. It doesn't attempt to fix PE artifacts — that crosses the separation-of-concerns boundary and risks introducing changes that the PE engine hasn't validated through its own governance cycle.

### Shared governance from PE v7

The following PE v7 design principles apply to article maintenance (not repeated in full — see [PE vision](../self-updating-prompt-engineering/20260428.01-vision.v7.md)):

- **Staleness avoidance first** — maintenance infrastructure is validated before it acts on articles
- **Human governance, autonomous execution** — humans set quality standards; the system executes maintenance
- **Minimal disruption** — quiet when articles are healthy, specific when they aren't
- **Scoped over exhaustive** — assess only articles with degradation signals, not the full inventory
- **Evidence-based** — every change cites its trigger, research findings, and validation scores
- **Graceful degradation** — if a source is unavailable, defer the check rather than guess
- **Iteration budget** — bounded autonomous article changes per cycle
- **Loop stability** — maintenance converges toward stable quality

## 🔍 What "self-updating article writing" means (and what it doesn't)

**It means:**

- Defining the quality standards (verified, reliable, readable, understandable, up to date) that articles must meet
- Defining the governance rules (autonomy gradient, cost stratification, series integrity) that maintenance must follow
- Continuously monitoring article freshness across the entire inventory
- Detecting broken links, stale facts, outdated code, and series inconsistencies
- Assessing quality through cost-stratified, per-dimension checks (Tier 0 → Tier 1 → Tier 2)
- Applying verified, change-scoped corrections autonomously for low-risk changes
- Escalating content-altering changes (factual rewrites, coverage additions) to human review
- Maintaining series coherence when individual articles are updated
- Signaling the PE engine when article outcomes suggest writing-rule issues
- Tracking outcomes to tune autonomy thresholds

**It doesn't mean:**

- Prescribing specific implementations — the vision defines standards, not tool selections
- Maintaining PE artifacts (instructions, context files, agents, prompts) — that's the PE engine's job
- Creating new articles from new knowledge — that's the research system's job
- Rewriting articles for stylistic preference — only degradation-driven fixes
- Modifying writing rules or standards — only signaling the PE engine when patterns suggest issues
- Guaranteeing zero staleness — some lag between degradation and detection is expected
- Replacing human editorial judgment — structural and strategic decisions remain human-only
- Verifying facts through LLM knowledge alone — source verification against current, accessible documents is required

## ⚠️ Risks and tensions

### Hallucinated corrections

The most dangerous failure mode: a builder capability "fixes" an article by introducing plausible but incorrect information. This is the article-writing equivalent of the research vision's existential hallucination risk. *Mitigation: R-A2 (verify before change) is unconditional. Research capability verifies facts against sources before builder capability applies changes. Post-change validation catches regressions. Factual changes are never autonomous — always at least notification level.*

### Over-maintenance

A system tuned to detect every imperfection generates noise and unnecessary changes. An article that's 92% of target readability doesn't need maintenance — one that's at 45% does. *Mitigation: freshness thresholds are set to trigger on meaningful degradation (score <50), not minor fluctuations. P4 findings (enhancements) are queued for scheduled reviews, not immediate action.*

### Series maintenance cascade

Updating one article in a series can trigger series-level validation, which finds issues in sibling articles, triggering their maintenance, which triggers more series-level checks. *Mitigation: iteration budget (default: 10 changes per cycle). Series-level validation runs once after all per-article changes in a cycle are applied, not after each individual change. Cascade depth is bounded.*

### Source availability

External sources cited in articles can become temporarily or permanently unavailable. A maintenance cycle can't verify facts against a source that's down. *Mitigation: graceful degradation — if a source is unavailable, the fact-check is deferred and the article is flagged for next-cycle re-check. Persistent unavailability (>3 cycles) escalates for human review of the citation.*

### Editorial voice erosion

Repeated autonomous maintenance can gradually shift an article's voice and style, even when individual changes are content-preserving. Each small change is acceptable; the cumulative effect may alter the article's character. *Mitigation: R-A3 (preserve editorial voice) constrains every change. Maintenance instructions to builder capabilities are change-scoped ("fix this specific link") not open-ended ("improve this article"). Article authors can mark sections as "editorial — do not modify" in comments.*

### PE artifact quality assumption

The system assumes that writing rules, validation criteria, and agent instructions are correct. If a PE artifact has a bug (e.g., validation criteria threshold is wrong), article maintenance will enforce that bug. *Mitigation: R-A6 (quality feedback to PE) detects patterns suggesting PE artifact issues and signals the PE engine. But the detection requires volume — a single incorrect validation threshold may not produce a detectable pattern until many articles are affected.*

### Freshness scoring limitations

The 5-signal freshness model is a proxy for article quality, not a direct measure. An article with perfect freshness score (recently validated, current versions, all links working, no issues, code passes) can still have factual errors or explanatory problems that the signals don't capture. *Mitigation: freshness scoring triggers assessment; it doesn't replace it. The assessment phase runs full 7-dimension validation regardless of what signal triggered it.*

### Cost of continuous monitoring

Freshness scoring across 100+ articles, regular link checking, and source monitoring consume tokens and network requests. *Mitigation: deterministic operations (link checking, freshness scoring) are cheap. LLM-assisted operations (source comparison, fact verification) run only on triggered articles. Validation caching (PE v7 R-S3) skips recently-validated articles.*

## ✅ Success criteria

The self-updating article-writing system succeeds when:

1. **Article freshness stays above threshold.** The percentage of articles with freshness score ≥50 stays above 90%. *Metric: weekly freshness score distribution across the article inventory.*

2. **Broken links are fixed within SLA.** P1 issues (broken links, broken code) are detected and fixed within 24 hours. *Metric: mean time from link breakage to fix.*

3. **Factual accuracy is maintained.** Articles reference current technology versions, working code examples, and valid sources. *Metric: percentage of articles passing factual accuracy validation dimension.*

4. **No hallucinated corrections.** Autonomous changes don't introduce factual errors. *Metric: zero CRITICAL findings introduced by autonomous changes (rolling 90 days). Any occurrence triggers immediate tightening of autonomy thresholds.*

5. **Series coherence is preserved.** When individual articles are updated, series-level dimensions (architecture, progression, terminology, structural echo) don't regress. *Metric: series-level validation pass rate after maintenance cycles.*

6. **Quality feedback loop produces signal.** Article outcome patterns that indicate PE artifact issues are detected and signaled to the PE engine. *Metric: count of actionable signals sent to PE engine per quarter.*

7. **Manual review burden decreases.** Over time, a larger proportion of maintenance actions proceed autonomously as trust is established. *Metric: ratio of autonomous to human-reviewed changes.*

8. **Article quality improves measurably.** Average validation scores across the article inventory trend upward over time. *Metric: per-dimension average scores per quarter.*
