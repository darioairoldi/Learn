## 🎯 The problem

The Learning Hub covers multiple technical domains — prompt engineering, technical writing, programming languages, authentication, Azure, data, hardware, and more. Each domain evolves through the same forces:

**Published knowledge expands.** New research papers, official documentation updates, tool releases, best practice guides, and community insights appear continuously. An article written six months ago may miss important developments.

**Understanding deepens.** As practitioners gain experience, the *way* a topic is best explained changes. What was once an advanced concept becomes foundational. What was best practice becomes anti-pattern.

**Cross-domain connections emerge.** Insights from one domain (e.g., prompt engineering's context rot research) inform another (e.g., technical writing's document structuring). These connections are invisible unless someone actively looks.

The outcome is familiar: **knowledge silently ages**. Articles don't become wrong overnight — they become incomplete, then outdated, then misleading. Manual monitoring across a dozen domains doesn't scale.

### Why this is different from PE self-update

The self-updating prompt engineering system (see [self-updating-prompt-engineering vision](../self-updating-prompt-engineering/06.000-vision.v6.md)) solves a *maintenance* problem: keeping known artifacts consistent with a changing platform. It starts from existing artifacts and checks them against reality.

Self-updating research solves a *discovery* problem: finding what's new, evaluating whether it matters, reasoning about its implications, and creating new knowledge artifacts. It starts from the outside world and works inward.

| Dimension | PE self-update | Research self-update |
|-----------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Starting point** | Known artifacts | Unknown landscape |
| **Primary signal** | Platform/ecosystem drift | New publications, discoveries, trends |
| **Core operation** | Validate and correct | Discover, reason, and synthesize |
| **Output** | Updated existing artifacts | New or substantially expanded articles |
| **Risk profile** | Structural breakage | Factual inaccuracy (hallucination) |
| **Verification** | Deterministic structural checks + LLM | Source triangulation + reasoning validation |

Despite these differences, the two systems share **governance infrastructure**: autonomy gradients, metadata contracts, multi-pass validation, decomposition, outcome logging, and loop stability. The vision inherits these proven patterns (see [Relationship to PE self-update](#relationship-to-pe-self-update)).

## 💡 The goal

Build a research automation system that **continuously discovers emerging knowledge** across configured subject domains, **validates claims against multiple independent sources**, **applies structured reasoning to extract actionable insights**, and **produces reliable, readable articles** — with the maximum degree of autonomy that assessed trustworthiness allows.

### The research cycle

The system performs a continuous cycle for each configured domain:

```
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                 │
    ▼                                                 │
┌─────────┐    ┌──────────┐    ┌─────────┐    ┌──────┴───┐
│ DISCOVER │───▶│ VALIDATE │───▶│ REASON  │───▶│SYNTHESIZE│
│          │    │          │    │         │    │          │
│ Search   │    │ Triangu- │    │ Deduce  │    │ Write    │
│ sources  │    │ late     │    │ Compare │    │ articles │
│ Extract  │    │ Grade    │    │ Predict │    │ Review   │
│ claims   │    │ Cross-   │    │ Connect │    │ Publish  │
│          │    │ check    │    │         │    │          │
└─────────┘    └──────────┘    └─────────┘    └──────────┘
```

### The trustworthiness gradient

Research autonomy is calibrated to **claim trustworthiness**, not just structural risk:

| Autonomy level | Scope | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Autonomous** | Established facts with ≥3 concordant sources | Updating version numbers, adding new official features documented in release notes |
| **Autonomous with review flag** | Well-sourced findings with 2 concordant sources | Adding a new section covering a recently published official guide |
| **Human review required** | Novel analysis, single-source claims, or deductive conclusions | Trend analysis, cross-domain connections, recommendations based on reasoning |
| **Human-only** | Strategic decisions, domain scope changes, editorial direction | Deciding which new domains to cover, changing the series architecture |

### Key definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Claim** | A specific factual assertion extracted from a source (e.g., "GPT-5.4 supports 1M+ token context windows") |
| **Source** | An identifiable origin of information with assessable credibility |
| **Triangulation** | Verification of a claim through ≥2 independent sources |
| **Grounding** | Linking every assertion in an article to its source claim(s) |
| **Confidence level** | Assessment of claim reliability: established (≥3 sources), supported (2 sources), emerging (1 credible source), speculative (reasoning-derived) |
| **Knowledge gap** | A topic within the domain's scope that existing articles don't cover or cover insufficiently |

## ⚙️ The rationale

### Why LLMs can do research (and why they're dangerous at it)

LLMs excel at the mechanical steps of research: searching, reading, extracting key information, comparing sources, identifying patterns, and writing clear prose. These capabilities make autonomous research *feasible*.

LLMs also hallucinate — they generate plausible-sounding claims that have no basis in their source material. They confidently cite papers that don't exist, attribute quotes to wrong authors, and invent statistics. This makes autonomous research *dangerous* without architectural safeguards.

The entire architecture is designed around one meta-principle: **every capability that makes LLMs useful for research also creates a hallucination vector that must be structurally mitigated**.

| LLM capability | Research value | Hallucination vector | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Web search** | Discovers new sources | May fabricate URLs or misread content | Verify every URL exists; re-read fetched content |
| **Text comprehension** | Extracts claims from documents | May misinterpret or insert claims not in the source | Extract with quotes; verify quotes exist in source text |
| **Comparison** | Identifies patterns across sources | May invent connections or overstate similarity | Require explicit evidence chains for every comparison |
| **Reasoning** | Draws conclusions from evidence | May generate plausible but unsupported deductions | Label reasoning confidence; require premises to be validated claims |
| **Writing** | Produces readable articles | May inject unsourced assertions during synthesis | Source-check every factual sentence against the validated claim set |

### Research-specific rationales

| ID | Rationale | Design implication |
|---|---|---|
| **R-R1-source-triangulation** | **No claim without triangulation.** A single source — no matter how credible — can be wrong, outdated, or misinterpreted. Two independent sources saying the same thing dramatically increases confidence. | Every factual claim requires ≥2 independent sources before it's treated as established. Single-source claims are labeled "emerging" and flagged for human attention. |
| **R-R2-grounded-writing** | **Every sentence traceable to evidence.** Articles must maintain a citation chain from each factual assertion back to validated claims, and from claims back to sources. | The synthesis phase produces articles with inline citations. A post-synthesis audit verifies no unsourced factual assertions remain. |
| **R-R3-reasoning-transparency** | **Deductive conclusions are labeled, not hidden.** When the system draws conclusions through reasoning (trend analysis, cross-domain connections, predictions), these must be explicitly labeled as analysis rather than presented as established facts. | Articles use distinct formatting for established facts vs. analytical conclusions. Reasoning chains are preserved in metadata for auditability. |
| **R-R4-source-credibility** | **Source quality matters more than source quantity.** Three blog posts don't outweigh one official specification. The system must assess source credibility, not just count sources. | Sources are classified by tier (official docs > peer-reviewed > verified community > unverified). | **R-R5-knowledge-continuity** | **New research builds on existing articles.** The system doesn't research in a vacuum — it knows what articles already exist, what they cover, and where gaps are. | Research cycles start with a knowledge map (existing articles + their coverage) and target gaps rather than re-researching known territory. |
| **R-R6-domain-configuration** | **Each domain has its own research profile.** Prompt engineering evolves weekly (platform releases). Programming languages evolve monthly (version releases). Authentication standards evolve yearly (RFCs). ## 🏗️ The vision

A self-updating research system **continuously monitors configured knowledge domains**, **discovers new information from credible sources**, **validates claims through triangulation**, **applies structured reasoning to identify actionable insights**, and **synthesizes reliable articles** — autonomously for well-sourced updates, with human review for novel analysis.

### Relationship to PE self-update

The PE self-update system and the research system are siblings, not parent-child:

- **PE self-update** maintains the *prompt engineering customization infrastructure* (agents, prompts, instructions, context files)
- **Research self-update** maintains the *published knowledge articles* across all domains

They share governance infrastructure but have distinct pipelines. When the research system discovers that prompt engineering knowledge has evolved, it updates *articles*. When the PE system detects that VS Code has changed, it updates *PE artifacts*. They can trigger each other: a PE discovery ("new YAML field available") can trigger a research task ("write about when to use it").

## 📋 Design principles

### Accuracy over speed

*The single most important principle.* A late article with verified claims is always better than a fast article with hallucinated claims. When time pressure and accuracy conflict, accuracy wins unconditionally.

### Source-grounded always

Every factual assertion in every article must be traceable to at least one validated source. The system never generates factual claims from its training data alone — it only reports what sources say and what can be logically deduced from source-backed premises.

### Confidence is explicit

Every claim, finding, and conclusion carries a confidence label:

| Confidence | Requirement | Presentation |
|---|---|---|
| **Established** | ≥3 concordant independent sources | Stated as fact |
| **Supported** | 2 concordant independent sources | Stated with implicit confidence |
| **Emerging** | 1 credible source | Flagged: "According to [source]..." |
| **Speculative** | Logical deduction from established/supported premises | Explicitly labeled as analysis |

### Reasoning is transparent

When the system draws conclusions through deduction, comparison, or trend analysis, the reasoning chain is preserved. The reader can trace *why* the system reached a conclusion, not just *what* it concluded. Deductive conclusions are presented in articles with distinct formatting (e.g., analysis sections, clearly marked as the author's interpretation).

### Domain-configured, not hardcoded

The system doesn't assume what's important for each domain. Source lists, research cadence, confidence thresholds, and scope boundaries are configured per domain. Adding a new domain means adding a configuration, not modifying the engine.

### Build on what exists

Research starts from the existing article inventory. The system maps what's already covered, identifies gaps relative to the current knowledge landscape, and focuses effort on gaps rather than re-researching known territory. This prevents both wasted effort and contradictions with existing content.

### Human governance, autonomous discovery

Humans define which domains to research, what sources to trust, and what confidence thresholds to apply. The system discovers, validates, and proposes autonomously within those boundaries. Novel analytical conclusions always surface to humans before publication.

### Shared governance from PE vision

The following PE design principles apply directly (not repeated in full — see [PE vision](../self-updating-prompt-engineering/06.000-vision.v6.md)):

- **Staleness avoidance first** — research infrastructure is validated before research runs
- **Metadata-driven** — articles carry structured metadata (goal, scope, sources, confidence)
- **Metadata-guarded changes** — article updates validated against declared scope before and after
- **Evidence-based** — every proposed change cites what triggered it and what evidence supports it
- **Graceful degradation** — if a source is unavailable, flag uncertainty rather than guess
- **Iteration budget** — bounded autonomous work per cycle
- **Loop stability** — convergence toward a stable knowledge state

## 🔬 The research pipeline

### Phase 1: Discover

*Relies on: R-R5-knowledge-continuity, R-R6-domain-configuration, R-L4-external-knowledge*

**Input:** Domain configuration + existing article inventory.

**Process:**
1. Build a **knowledge map** — what existing articles cover, when they were last updated, what sources they cite
2. Search configured sources for content published since the last research cycle
3. Search for emerging topics via broader web research within domain scope
4. Extract **candidate claims** — specific factual assertions from each source, with verbatim quotes where possible
5. Identify **knowledge gaps** — topics within scope that existing articles don't cover

**Output:** A structured list of candidate claims with source URLs, publication dates, and relevance assessments.

**Anti-hallucination:** Every candidate claim is linked to a specific URL + quote. No claim enters the pipeline without a verifiable source reference.

### Phase 2: Validate

*Relies on: R-R1-source-triangulation, R-R4-source-credibility, R-L2-self-correction*

**Input:** Candidate claims from Phase 1.

**Process:**
1. For each candidate claim, **search for independent corroboration** — at least one additional source that confirms the claim without citing the original
2. **Grade source credibility** — official docs > peer-reviewed > established community > blog posts > unknown
3. **Cross-check for contradictions** — do any credible sources dispute the claim?
4. **Assign confidence level** — established/supported/emerging based on source count and credibility
5. **Flag low-confidence claims** for human review rather than including in articles

**Output:** A validated claim set with confidence levels, source chains, and contradiction notes.

**Anti-hallucination:** Triangulation is the primary defense. A hallucinated claim won't have corroborating sources. Contradictions between sources surface uncertain areas rather than hiding them.

### Phase 3: Reason

*Relies on: R-R3-reasoning-transparency, R-L1-llm-reasoning*

**Input:** Validated claim set from Phase 2.

**Process:**
1. **Compare** — how do validated findings relate to existing article content? What's new, what's changed, what's been invalidated?
2. **Deduce** — what logically follows from the validated claim set? ("If A and B are true, then C follows")
3. **Connect** — are there cross-domain implications? (A prompt engineering finding that affects technical writing guidance)
4. **Trend** — do multiple findings point in the same direction? (Several sources suggesting a shift in best practices)
5. **Prioritize** — which findings are most impactful for the domain's audience?

**Output:** A reasoning report with prioritized findings, deductive conclusions (with explicit premise chains), and cross-domain connections.

**Anti-hallucination:** Every deductive conclusion must cite its premises, and every premise must be a validated claim. Speculative analysis is explicitly labeled. The reasoning chain is auditable.

### Phase 4: Synthesize

*Relies on: R-R2-grounded-writing, article-writing.instructions.md*

**Input:** Reasoning report + existing articles + article-writing standards.

**Process:**
1. **Plan articles** — determine whether to update existing articles or create new ones
2. **Draft content** — write following the article-writing instructions (sentence case, contractions, emoji H2s, Diátaxis type, etc.)
3. **Embed citations** — every factual sentence links to its source claim(s)
4. **Post-draft audit** — an independent LLM pass checks every factual assertion against the validated claim set. Unsourced assertions are flagged and removed.
5. **Metadata update** — article metadata reflects new sources, updated scope, current date

**Output:** Draft articles ready for human review.

**Anti-hallucination:** The post-draft audit is the final defense. It treats the draft as an adversarial document and asks: "can every factual sentence be justified by the validated claim set?" Sentences that can't are removed, even if they sound correct.

### Phase 5: Review and publish

*Relies on: R-G1-autonomy-gradient*

**Input:** Draft articles from Phase 4.

**Process:**
1. **Autonomous publication** (future, after trust is established) — articles with only established/supported claims, updating existing content within declared scope
2. **Human review required** (default) — articles with emerging claims, new topics, deductive conclusions, or scope expansions

**Output:** Published or queued-for-review articles.

## 🛡️ Hallucination resistance architecture

Hallucination resistance isn't a single check — it's layered throughout the pipeline:

| Layer | Where | What it catches | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| **L1: Source verification** | Phase 1 | Fabricated URLs, misattributed content | Fetch every URL; verify content matches claimed extract |
| **L2: Claim triangulation** | Phase 2 | Single-source claims that may be errors | Require ≥2 independent sources for "supported" confidence |
| **L3: Contradiction detection** | Phase 2 | Claims disputed by other credible sources | Cross-check candidate claims against each other and existing articles |
| **L4: Premise validation** | Phase 3 | Reasoning from false premises | Every deductive premise must be a validated claim |
| **L5: Reasoning audit** | Phase 3 | Logical leaps, overstatement, false patterns | Independent LLM pass reviews reasoning chains |
| **L6: Draft audit** | Phase 4 | Unsourced assertions injected during writing | Independent LLM pass checks every factual sentence against claim set |
| **L7: Confidence labeling** | Phase 4 | Mixing speculation with established facts | Distinct formatting for established facts, emerging findings, and analysis |

**No single layer is sufficient.** The architecture assumes each layer will miss some hallucinations. The layers are designed so that what L1 misses, L2 catches; what L2 misses, L6 catches. The cumulative effect is high reliability.

### What this architecture cannot catch

- **Coordinated misinformation** — if multiple independent sources all state the same incorrect claim (e.g., a widely reported but incorrect statistic), triangulation will validate it. *Mitigation: prefer official sources; flag claims that appear suddenly across many sources.*
- **Subtle misinterpretation** — the LLM may correctly fetch a source but subtly misunderstand its nuance. *Mitigation: extract verbatim quotes; multi-pass review.*
- **Outdated triangulation** — two sources from 2024 may agree on something that changed in 2026. *Mitigation: weight recent sources higher; check publication dates.*

## 🔍 What "self-updating research" means (and what it doesn't)

**It means:**

- Continuously scanning configured sources for new information in each domain
- Extracting and validating claims through source triangulation
- Identifying what's new, what's changed, and what matters for the audience
- Applying structured reasoning to extract insights and connections
- Producing article drafts that follow established writing standards
- Maintaining citation chains from articles back to original sources
- Learning from outcomes (which research approaches produce the best articles)

**It doesn't mean:**

- Generating "content" from LLM training data without source verification
- Replacing human editorial judgment for strategic decisions
- Automatically trusting every source equally
- Publishing without human review (until trust is established through outcome data)
- Covering every possible topic — scope is configured per domain
- Real-time news coverage — research operates on configured cadences (weekly/monthly)
- Guaranteeing zero errors — the goal is *high* reliability, not perfection

## ⚠️ Risks and tensions

### Hallucination as existential risk

A PE artifact with a bug produces bad prompts. An article with hallucinated facts produces misinformation. The consequences are categorically different. This is why the hallucination resistance architecture has seven layers rather than one, and why the default publication mode requires human review.

### Source availability and stability

Web sources disappear, move, or change. A claim validated today may have a broken source link tomorrow. *Mitigation: archive source content at validation time; log source URLs with content hashes; detect and flag broken source chains during maintenance cycles.*

### Reasoning overreach

LLMs are good at pattern matching but can overstate connections. "Three sources mention X" doesn't mean X is a trend — it may be three sources citing the same original. *Mitigation: independent source verification; explicit confidence labeling; human review for all deductive conclusions.*

### Coverage vs. depth trade-off

A dozen domains each needing weekly research creates volume pressure. Volume pressure encourages shallow coverage. *Mitigation: domain-specific cadences (fast-moving domains get more frequent cycles); depth-over-breadth as a default; iteration budgets prevent runaway cycles.*

### Cost of thoroughness

Seven-layer hallucination resistance means multiple LLM passes per claim. Triangulation means searching for corroboration for every candidate claim. This is expensive. *Mitigation: R-P2-decomposition (focus on high-value claims); R-S3-validation-caching (skip recently-validated territory); tiered processing (official sources get fast-track; unknown sources get full validation).*

### False confidence from process rigor

A sophisticated multi-layer validation process can create false confidence: "if it passed seven checks, it must be true." No. The checks reduce error probability; they don't eliminate it. *Mitigation: confidence levels are always explicit; the system never claims certainty, only assessed probability.*

### Cross-domain coherence

When the research system updates articles in one domain, those updates may affect articles in another domain. *Mitigation: cross-reference checking during synthesis; flagging when a finding in domain A contradicts existing content in domain B.*

## ✅ Success criteria

The self-updating research system succeeds when:

1. **Knowledge coverage improves measurably.** Articles cover topics that would have been missed without autonomous research. *Metric: number of knowledge gaps identified and filled per cycle.*

2. **Factual accuracy remains high.** Published articles have a low rate of corrections needed after human review. *Metric: percentage of claims that survive human review without modification.*

3. **Source quality is verifiable.** Every factual claim in every article can be traced to its source(s). *Metric: percentage of factual sentences with valid citation chains.*

4. **Research cadence matches domain velocity.** Fast-moving domains (prompt engineering) are reviewed weekly; slower domains (authentication standards) monthly. *Metric: time from source publication to article update.*

5. **Human review burden decreases over time.** As the system demonstrates reliability, more categories of updates can proceed with review-flag rather than review-required. *Metric: ratio of autonomous-with-flag to human-required articles over time.*

6. **False claims caught before publication.** The hallucination resistance layers catch errors before they reach human reviewers. *Metric: number of hallucinated claims caught by L1–L6 before reaching human review.*

7. **Articles meet writing standards.** Research-produced articles pass the same quality checks as manually written articles. *Metric: article-review validation scores for research-produced vs. manually written articles.*

8. **Cost per article is bounded.** Token consumption per research cycle doesn't grow unbounded as domains expand. *Metric: estimated token cost per domain per cycle.*
