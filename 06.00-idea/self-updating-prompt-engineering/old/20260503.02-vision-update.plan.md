# Vision Update Plan: Elevate Modular Review and Progressive Assessment to Foundational Status

> Make the vision's most important efficiency and modularity decisions visible at the strategic level — not just in Assess-layer details.

---

## Priority analysis: What matters most for peak quality at minimal cost

### The prioritization criterion

Every element in the vision's foundational sections should be weighted by its contribution to:
1. **Peak artifact quality** (reliability, effectiveness, efficiency) — the primary goal
2. **Minimal update cost** — the constraint that makes the goal sustainable

Elements that directly serve both (quality AND cost) deserve highest visibility. Elements that serve quality but not cost (or vice versa) deserve clear presence. Mechanism details that explain HOW rather than WHAT/WHY deserve presence in the Rationale or Vision layers, not the Goal.

**The imbalance**: Breaking/non-breaking classification takes **45% of the Goal** with tables, multi-dimensional impact analysis, behavioral compatibility layers, confidence factors, and N-1 classification steps. This is essential content — but it's HOW changes are classified (mechanism), not WHAT the system achieves (goal) or WHY it's built this way (rationale). Meanwhile, the two concepts that directly enable "peak quality at minimal cost" (progressive assessment and modular invocation) are absent from the Goal entirely.

## Proposed vision changes (v9 → v9.1)

### Change V17 (✅ Done): Add cost-of-detection to the Problem statement

**Location**: Vision § The problem, new paragraph after "Capability/logic staleness (Type B)"

**Proposed addition**:

> **The cost challenge.** Detecting drift — especially Type B — requires comparing artifacts against external reality. For a system with dozens of artifacts across multiple types, full-depth assessment of every artifact on every review cycle is prohibitively expensive. The cost grows with the number of artifacts × the number of external change sources × the depth of the dependency chain. A sustainable self-update system must bound this cost without sacrificing detection quality — assessing deeply where it matters and lightly where it doesn't, guided by what's actually changed in the world.

**Rationale**: The Problem describes *what* goes wrong but not *why detecting it at scale is hard*. The cost challenge is the motivating problem that progressive depth solves — without it in the Problem, progressive depth looks like an optimization rather than a necessity.

**Classification**: EXTENDING.

### Change V18 (✅ Done): Extend the Goal with cost-bounded operation and modular invocation

**Location**: Vision § The goal, extend "Two dimensions" + add third prerequisite

**Extend "Two dimensions"**:

> Current: "…and efficient (not re-validating unchanged artifacts or consuming tokens on low-value checks)."
>
> Proposed: "…and efficient (assessing deeply where changes warrant it and lightly where they don't — bounded by a progressive cost gradient from zero-cost cache hits through lightweight screening to full-depth review, never applying more assessment than the evidence requires)."

**Add third prerequisite**:

> 3\. **Modular, type-aware invocation.** The system can be invoked for specific artifact types — "review my agents", "update context files" — not just "review everything." Each invocation assesses the target type deeply while screening its dependencies lightly, escalating to deep assessment only when screening detects issues. This keeps the cost of any single invocation proportional to the work that actually needs doing, not the size of the entire artifact set.

**Rationale**: The Goal says "efficient" but only as a negative ("not re-validating"). Progressive depth is the positive definition. Modular invocation is as fundamental as the other prerequisites.

### Change V20 (✅ Done): Extend the Vision statement with modular and progressive concepts

**Location**: Vision § The vision, new paragraph after engine/integration separation

**Proposed addition**:

> The system operates **modularly and progressively**. It can be invoked for a specific artifact type — reviewing agents without full-depth analysis of every context file they depend on. Assessment proceeds in three phases: research gathers what's changed in the world (once per invocation), informed screening checks dependencies cheaply (deterministic + lightweight relevance), and deep pass applies full quality requirements only to targets and dependencies that screening flags. This progressive model keeps each invocation cost-bounded while preserving detection quality — the system never applies more assessment than the evidence requires.

**Rationale**: The Vision statement describes what the system does and how it's architected, but not how it controls cost or supports modular use.

### Change V22 (✅ Done): Relocate classification mechanics from Goal to Rationale

**Location**: Vision § The goal (breaking/non-breaking + classification in practice) → Vision § The rationale (new subsection)

**What stays in Goal** (replace current 45 lines with ~10-line summary):

> ### Change classification
>
> The autonomy level for any change is determined by **assessed impact × confidence**. A **non-breaking** change preserves the artifact's declared goal, scope, and boundaries. A **breaking** change alters them. Impact is multi-dimensional: consistency, efficiency, behavioral compatibility, and effectiveness are each assessed independently — the highest-risk dimension governs.
>
> Confidence increases when classification is machine-verifiable: metadata comparison (R-S1), structural separation labels (R-P4), and historical success rates (R-G3). Without metadata and N-1 labels, all classification falls to LLM judgment — the most expensive and least confident path.
>
> **Full classification mechanics**: See The rationale § Change classification mechanics.

**What moves to Rationale** (new subsection after the governance model):

> ### Change classification mechanics
>
> [The current "Breaking vs. non-breaking classification" content: impact table, multi-dimensional assessment, behavioral compatibility layers, confidence factors]
>
> [The current "How classification works in practice" content: N-1 classification path, deterministic steps 1-4]

**Rationale**: The Goal section should answer "WHAT does the system achieve?" — not "HOW does it classify changes?" The classification mechanism is important (it enables the autonomy gradient) but it's a rationale-level concern. Currently it takes 45% of the Goal, crowding out the concepts that matter most for peak quality at minimal cost (progressive assessment, modular invocation). Relocating the detail preserves every word — it just moves it to where mechanics belong.

**Classification**: RESTRUCTURING — relocates content without removing it. Requires explicit acknowledgment that this is a structural change, not just an extension.

## Why this matters

The Goal section is the first thing a reader encounters after the Problem. Its current content allocation sends the wrong priority signal:

| What the Goal emphasizes | % of Goal | What it should emphasize |
|---|---|---|
| Classification mechanics | 45% | ~10% (summary + pointer to Rationale) |
| Guidance quality | 18% | 18% (correct) |
| Autonomy gradient | 10% | 10% (correct) |
| Progressive assessment | 0% | ~15% (cost-bounded efficiency) |
| Modular invocation | 0% | ~10% (prerequisite #3) |
| Cost challenge | 0% | Belongs in Problem, not Goal |

After V17-V22, the Goal will lead with what matters most: peak quality (guidance prerequisite), bounded cost (progressive assessment), and practical usability (modular invocation). The classification mechanics — essential but secondary — will be summarized in the Goal and detailed in the Rationale where mechanism explanations belong.
